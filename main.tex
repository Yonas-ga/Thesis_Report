\noindent In the following, let $p$ be a fixed prime number. 

\noindent It is common knowledge that $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}/(p)$ endowed with the usual addition and multiplication modulo $p$ is a field with a finite amount of elements. This can easily be checked, but can also be proven using the fact that the ideal $(p)$ is maximal, and that the quotient of a ring with a maximal ideal is a field.  \textbf{Maybe proof in appendix ? not sure its necessary}

\noindent Now recall the construction of the frobenius map, a fundamental map that appears in every ring of characteristic $p$: $$F: A \to A$$ defined by $$F(x) = x^p$$ for any ring $A$ of characteristic $p$.

\noindent This ring homomorphism is well defined. Indeed, for all $x,y\in A$, one has:

$$F(xy) = (xy)^p = x^p y^p = F(x)F(y)$$
\noindent And also $$F(x+y) = (x+y)^p$$
\noindent Where we can utilize the fact that in modulo $p$, $\forall a,b\in \mathbb{F}_p$ we have $(a+b)^p=a^p+b^p$. In fact, $$(a+b)^p = \sum_{i=0}^{p}\binom{p}{i}a^ib^{p-i} = a^p+b^p+\sum_{i=1}^{p-1}\binom{p}{i}a^ib^{p-i}$$
\noindent And the binomial factor $\binom{p}{i}$ is a multiple of $p$ for all $i\in\{1,\ldots,p-1\}$, so it is nul in modulo $p$, so
$$(x+y)^p = x^p + y^p$$
Therefore $$F(x+y)=F(x)+F(y)$$
And the frobenius map is a ring homomorphism. 

\noindent In fact, in the special case where $A=\mathbb{F}_p$, we get that the frobenius map is the identity. 

\noindent Let $a\in \mathbb{F}_p$. If $a=0$ then the identity holds. Else, $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}= \mathbb{F}_p\backslash\{0\}$
and then, by Fermats little theorem, $$a^p\equiv a \mod p$$
Now because we work in characteristic $p$, this congruence is in fact an equality, and the frobenius map is the identity.

\noindent From this, if $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$ we get that $a^{p-1}\equiv1\mod p$ by multplying both sides by $a^{-1}$. This means that each non-zero element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ is a $(p-1)$th root of unity.

\noindent In the following, let $p$ be a fixed prime number. 

\noindent  Every $p$-adic integer $s \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ admits a unique expansion of the form $$s=\sum_{i=0}^{\infty}a_ip^i \text{  with  } a_i\in\{0,..,p-1\}$$

\noindent Since the set $\{0,1,\ldots,p-1\}$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{F}_p$, each $p$-adic number can be uniquely represented by a sequence $(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{F}_p$.

\noindent This suggests that there may exist a way of reconstructing $\mathbb{Z}_p$ from $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$. We now investigate whether such a reconstruction can be made into a ring-theoretic one.

\noindent Since $\mathbb{Z}_p$ is a ring, we need to endow $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ with a ring structure that support the arithmetic of $p$-adic integers. However this cannot be done using componentwise operations because of the carry arithmetic involved in addition and multiplication of $p$-adic integers. For example: \newline $$(1,0,0,\ldots)+(p-1,0,0,\ldots)=(0,0,0,\ldots)$$ under componentwise addition in $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ whereas $$1+p-1=1*p$$ in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ corresponds to $$(0,1,0,\ldots)\ne (0,0,0,\ldots)$$

\noindent Thus the correct ring structure on $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ cannot be the naive componentwise one.


\noindent A first idea would be to define a map
$$\phi:\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}\to\mathbb{Z}_p $$
such that: $$\phi((a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots))=a_0+a_1p+a_2p^2+\ldots$$
but such a construction is not yet well-defined, since the coefficients $a_i$ lie in $\mathbb{F}_p$, whereas the series is an element of $\mathbb{Z}_p$. 
So first, we must first choose a lift of the coefficients from $\mathbb{F}_p$ to $\mathbb{Z}_p$.

\noindent A natural choice is given by the Teichmüller representatives.
\newline
\newline
\newline

\noindent A natural choice is given by the Teichmüller representatives. For each \(a \in \mathbb{F}_p\), there exists a unique lift \(\chi(a) \in \mathbb{Z}_p\) such that
\[
\chi(a) \equiv a \pmod p
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\chi(a)^p = \chi(a).
\]
These representatives are canonical and are fixed by Frobenius. We may therefore replace the previous expression by
\[
\phi((a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)) = \chi(a_0) + \chi(a_1)p + \chi(a_2)p^2 + \cdots.
\]
This gives a meaningful way to associate a \(p\)-adic integer to a sequence of elements of \(\mathbb{F}_p\).
