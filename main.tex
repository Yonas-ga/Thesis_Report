\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}
Witt vector rings are algebraic objects with multiple useful properties such as the possibilty of \emph{lifting} rings from characteristic $p$, with $p$ a prime, to characteristic $0$, as well as being an alternative way of constructing \emph{p-adic numbers}. 

Computationally we use truncated Witt vectors, the finite counterpart to Witt vectors, as it has the same properties in the finite case. The problem is that the construction of the Witt vector polynomials are of exponential complexity, both in $p$ and in the lenght of the trunacated Witt vectors, called \emph{precision}.

So caching etc is important.

\subsection{Mathematical background}\label{subsec:background}

In the following, let $p$ be a fixed prime number. 

It is common knowledge that $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}/(p)$ endowed with the usual addition and multiplication modulo $p$ is a field with a finite amount of elements. This can easily be checked, and can also be proven using the fact that the ideal $(p)$ is maximal, and that the quotient of a ring with a maximal ideal is a field.  \textbf{Maybe proof in appendix ? not sure its necessary}

Now recall the construction of the Frobenius map, a fundamental map that appears in every ring $A$ of characteristic $p$: \[F: A \to A\] defined by \[F(x) = x^p\] for any ring $A$ of characteristic $p$.

This ring homomorphism is well defined. Indeed, for all $x,y\in A$, one has:

\[F(xy) = (xy)^p = x^p y^p = F(x)F(y).\]
And also \[F(x+y) = (x+y)^p.\]
Where we can utilize the fact that in modulo $p$, for all $a,b\in \mathbb{F}_p$ we have $(a+b)^p=a^p+b^p$. In fact, \[(a+b)^p = \sum_{i=0}^{p}\binom{p}{i}a^ib^{p-i} = a^p+b^p+\sum_{i=1}^{p-1}\binom{p}{i}a^ib^{p-i}.\]
And the binomial factor $\binom{p}{i}$ is a multiple of $p$ for all $i\in\{1,\ldots,p-1\}$, so it is zero in modulo $p$, so
\[(x+y)^p = x^p + y^p.\]
Therefore \[F(x+y)=F(x)+F(y).\]
And the Frobenius map is a ring homomorphism. 

In fact, in the special case where $A=\mathbb{F}_p$, we get that the Frobenius map is the identity. 

Let $a\in \mathbb{F}_p$. If $a=0$ then the identity holds. Else, $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}= \mathbb{F}_p\backslash\{0\}$
and then, by Fermat's little theorem, \[a^p\equiv a \pmod p.\]
Since $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$, congruence modulo $p$ becomes equality, and the Frobenius map is indeed the identity.

From this, if $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$ we get that $a^{p-1}\equiv1\pmod p$ by multplying both sides by $a^{-1}$. This means that each non-zero element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ is a $(p-1)$th root of unity.

\begin{theorem}[Hensel's lemma]\label{Hensel}
Let $f(X)\in\mathbb{Z}_p[X]$, and let $a_0\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ be such that
\[f(a_0)\equiv 0 \pmod p \qquad\text{and}\qquad f'(a_0)\not\equiv 0 \pmod p.\]
Then there exists a unique $\alpha\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ such that
\[ \alpha\equiv a_0 \pmod p \qquad\text{and}\qquad f(\alpha)=0.\]
\end{theorem}
The proof of this theorem is long, and out of scope. It can be found in the first 3 pages of (reference) if the reader is interested.

\subsection{As mathetical objects}
Why ? How ? Construction from basic math and up.

There are multiple ways of introducing Witt vectors. Most references state the definition of Witt vectors and work with them directly. We will instead construct Witt vectors from scratch, to try and make sense of the definition and understand the object.

We will first show how the idea of Witt vectors come to light naturally, then how one comes to the idea of ghost components, finally we will show how the operations result in sum and product polynomials.
\newline
\newline
In the following, let $p$ be a fixed prime number. 

Every $p$-adic integer $s \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ admits a unique expansion of the form \[s=\sum_{i=0}^{\infty}a_ip^i \text{  with  } a_i\in\{0,..,p-1\}.\]

Since the set $\{0,1,\ldots,p-1\}$ is bijective to $\mathbb{F}_p$, each $p$-adic number can be uniquely represented by a sequence $(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{F}_p$.

This suggests that there may exist a way of reconstructing $\mathbb{Z}_p$ from $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$. We now investigate whether such a reconstruction can be made into a ring-theoretic one.

Since $\mathbb{Z}_p$ is a ring, we need to endow $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ with a ring structure that support the arithmetic of $p$-adic integers. However this cannot be done using componentwise operations because of the carry arithmetic involved in addition and multiplication of $p$-adic integers. For example:
\[(1,0,0,\ldots)+(p-1,0,0,\ldots)=(0,0,0,\ldots)\] under componentwise addition in $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ whereas \[1+(p-1)=p\] in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ corresponds to \[(0,1,0,\ldots)\ne (0,0,0,\ldots).\]

Thus the correct ring structure on $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ cannot be the naive componentwise one.


A first idea would be to define a map
\[\phi:\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}\to\mathbb{Z}_p \]
such that: \[\phi((a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots))=a_0+a_1p+a_2p^2+\ldots\]
but such a construction is not yet well-defined, since the coefficients $a_i$ lie in $\mathbb{F}_p$, whereas the series is an element of $\mathbb{Z}_p$. 

We must therefore choose, for each coefficient in $\mathbb{F}_p$, a canonical lift to $\mathbb{Z}_p$. In other words, we seek a map \[\chi:\mathbb{F}_p\to\mathbb{Z}_p\]
which is a section (ie. some kind of inverse on $\mathbb{F}_p$) of the reduction map \[\pi:\mathbb{Z}_p\to\mathbb{F}_p,\qquad \pi(x)=x \bmod p\]
so that \[\pi(\chi(a))=a \qquad\text{for all } a\in\mathbb{F}_p.\]
Equivalently, we require \[ \chi(a)\equiv a \pmod p.\]

In order for this lift to interact well with the arithmetic in characteristic $p$, it is natural to impose the additional condition that $\chi(a)$ be fixed by Frobenius: \[\chi(a)^p=\chi(a).\]
Thus we are now looking for elements $x\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ satisfying $x^p=x$.

Solving this equation gets us: \[x^p=x \Longleftrightarrow x(x^{p-1}-1)=0.\]
That is, $x$ is either $0$ or a $(p-1)$th root of unity.

In fact such a lift exists, and it is unique. 

\begin{proposition}
For every $a\in\mathbb{F}_p$, there exists a unique element $\chi(a)\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ such that
\[\chi(a)\equiv a \pmod p \qquad \text{and} \qquad \chi(a)^p=\chi(a).\]
This map $\chi:\mathbb{F}_p\to\mathbb{Z}_p$ is called the Teichmüller lift.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First consider the case $a=0$. Then $x=0$ clearly satisfies
\[ x\equiv 0 \pmod p\qquad\text{and}\qquad x^p=x\]
and it is the unique such element.

Now let $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^\times$. We look for $x\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ such that
\[x\equiv a \pmod p \qquad\text{and}\qquad x^p=x.\]
Since $x$ is nonzero, the equation $x^p=x$ is equivalent to \[x^{p-1}=1.\]
Now, consider the polynomial \[f(X)=X^{p-1}-1\in\mathbb{Z}_p[X].\]
Because $a\in\mathbb{F}_p^\times$, from Subsecton \ref{subsec:background} we get that 
\[f(a)=a^{p-1}-1=0\equiv 0 \pmod p.\]
Moreover, \[f'(X)=(p-1)X^{p-2}\]
so \[ f'(a)=(p-1)a^{p-2}\not\equiv 0 \pmod p\]
since $a\neq 0$ and $p-1\not\equiv 0\pmod p$.

Therefore, by Hensel’s lemma \ref{Hensel}, there exists a unique $\widetilde a\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ such that
\[\widetilde a\equiv a \pmod p \qquad\text{and}\qquad f(\widetilde a)=0\]
And so, \[f(\widetilde a)=\widetilde a^{p-1}-1= 0\implies \widetilde a^{p-1}=1.\]
Since $\widetilde a\neq 0$, multiplying both sides by $\widetilde a$ yields \[\widetilde a^p=\widetilde a.\]
We now define $\chi(a):=\widetilde a$.

This proves that for every $a\in\mathbb{F}_p$ there exists a unique element $\chi(a)\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ satisfying
\[\chi(a)\equiv a \pmod p \qquad\text{and}\qquad \chi(a)^p=\chi(a)\].
\end{proof}

We may now define
\[\phi((a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)) = \chi(a_0)+\chi(a_1)p+\chi(a_2)p^2+\cdots\]
which is a well-defined element of $\mathbb{Z}_p$. Thus every sequence in $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ gives rise to a $p$-adic expansion with Teichmüller representatives as coefficients.

The map $\phi$ provides a bijection between $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ and $\mathbb{Z}_p$. One might then attempt to transport the ring structure of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ to $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ with $\phi$. 
In other words, for two sequences \[a=(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots), \qquad b=(b_0,b_1,b_2,\ldots), \qquad a,b\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}\]
we may define \[a \oplus b := \phi^{-1}(\phi(a)+\phi(b)), \qquad a \otimes b := \phi^{-1}(\phi(a)\phi(b)).\]

This indeed defines a ring structure on $\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ that is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_p$. However, the resulting formulas for the coordinates of $a\oplus b$ and $a\otimes b$ are complicated due to the carry terms appearing in the arithmetic of $p$-adic expansions.

To understand this phenomenon more precisely, let \[x=\phi(a), \qquad y=\phi(b).\]
Then \[x=\chi(a_0)+p\chi(a_1)+p^2\chi(a_2)+\cdots\]
and \[y=\chi(b_0)+p\chi(b_1)+p^2\chi(b_2)+\cdots.\]

Now let $c\in\mathbb{F}_p^{\mathbb{N}}$ such that \[ x+y=\phi(c)=\chi(c_0)+p\chi(c_1)+p^2\chi(c_2)+\cdots.\]
This then gives the following congruences:
\begin{equation}\label{original_congruence}
\begin{aligned}
\chi(c_0) &\equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) \pmod{p} \\
\chi(c_0) + \chi(c_1)p &\equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(a_1)p + \chi(b_0) + \chi(b_1)p \pmod{p^2} \\
\chi(c_0) + \chi(c_1)p + \chi(c_2)p^2 &\equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(a_1)p + \chi(a_2)p^2  + \chi(b_0) + \chi(b_1)p + \chi(b_2)p^2 \pmod{p^3} \\
&\vdots
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The first congruence directly implies that $c_0=a_0+b_0$.

Now we will prove the following lemma, which will help solve for $c_i$ for $i>1$.

\begin{lemma}\label{equiv+i}
For $x,y\in \mathbb{F}_p$, If $x\equiv y \pmod p$ then $x^{p^i}\equiv y^{p^i} \pmod {p^{i+1}}$ for every $i\in\mathbb{N}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  We will proceed by recursion on $i\in\mathbb{N}$, with the case $i=0$ being the hypothesis.
  Suppose now that for $i\in\mathbb{N}$ we have $x^{p^i}\equiv y^{p^i} \pmod p^{i+1}$. We can write \[x^{p^i}=y^{p^i}p^{i+1}\implies x^{p^{i+1}}=\bigl(y^{p^i}p^{i+1}\bigr)^p.\]
  Now expanding with Newton's binomial theorem, \[x^{p^{i+1}}=\bigl(y^{p^i}p^{i+1}\bigr)^p=y^{p^i}+p^{p(i+1)}+\sum_{k=0}^{p-1}\binom{p}{k}y^{p^{i}(p-k)}p^{k(i+1)}.\]
  For $k\in\{1,\ldots,p-1\}$ we have \[\binom{p}{k}\mid p \implies \binom{p}{k}y^{p^{i}(p-k)}p^{p(i+1)}\mid p^{i+2}.\]
  And \[p^{p(i+1)}\mid p^{i+2} \text{ since }p\geq 2.\]
  We indeed get that $x^{p^{i+1}}\equiv y^{p^{i+1}} \pmod {p^{i+2}}$.
\end{proof}
Trying to find $c_1$ using the congruence above, we get \[\chi(c_1)p \equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(a_1)p + \chi(b_0) + \chi(b_1)p - \chi(c_0) \pmod{p^2}.\]

Using that $\chi(c_0) \equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) \pmod{p}$ we get that $\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) - \chi(c_0)  \mid p$ so we can divide by $p$ \begin{equation}\chi(c_1) \equiv  \chi(a_1) +  \chi(b_1) + \frac{\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) - \chi(c_0)}{p}\pmod{p}.\end{equation}\label{congruence}
Now using the lemma \ref{equiv+i} and the properties of the Teichmüller lift, we get that: \[\chi(c_0) \equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) \pmod{p} \implies \chi(c_0)^p \equiv \bigl(\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0)\bigr)^p \pmod{p^2}\]\[ \implies \chi(c_0) \equiv \bigl(\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0)\bigr)^p \pmod{p^2} \]
We will expand Newtons binomial $\bigl(\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0)\bigr)^p=\chi(a_0)^p+\chi(b_0)^p+\sum_{k=1}^{p-1}\binom{p}{k}\chi(a_0)^k\chi(b_0)^{p-k}$. 
Now note that \[\binom{p}{k}=\frac{p!}{k!(p-k)!}\implies \binom{p}{k}\mid p \text{ and } \binom{p}{k}\nmid p^2 \text{ for } k\in\{1,\ldots,p-1\}.\]
So \[\chi(c_0) \equiv \chi(a_0)+\chi(b_0)+p(\chi(a_0)^{p-1}\chi(b_0)+\ldots+\chi(a_0)\chi(b_0)^{p-1}) \pmod{p^2}.\]
Reordering gets us \[\chi(a_0)+\chi(b_0)-\chi(c_0)\equiv -p(\chi(a_0)^{p-1}\chi(b_0)+\ldots+\chi(a_0)\chi(b_0)^{p-1})\pmod{p^2}.\]
Again, since  $\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) - \chi(c_0)  \mid p$, \[\frac{\chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) - \chi(c_0)}{p}\equiv -(\chi(a_0)^{p-1}\chi(b_0)+\ldots+\chi(a_0)\chi(b_0)^{p-1})\pmod{p}\]
Now injecting this into \ref{congruence} gets us \[\chi(c_1) \equiv  \chi(a_1) +  \chi(b_1) -(\chi(a_0)^{p-1}\chi(b_0)+\ldots+\chi(a_0)\chi(b_0)^{p-1}) \pmod{p}\]
\[c_1 \equiv a_1 + b_1 -(a_0^{p-1}b_0+\ldots+a_0b_0^{p-1}) \pmod{p}.\]
So \[c_1 = a_1 + b_1 -(a_0^{p-1}b_0+\ldots+a_0b_0^{p-1})\] since we have elements in $\mathbb{F}_p$.

As you can see, this computation is quite heavy because of the \emph{carry term}, and it becomes even more complicated when computing more. Instead, one could go back to \ref{original_congruence} and use the lemma \ref{equiv+i} to get:
\begin{align*}
\chi(c_0) &\equiv \chi(a_0) + \chi(b_0) \pmod{p} \\
\chi(c_0)^p + \chi(c_1)p &\equiv \chi(a_0)^p + \chi(a_1)p + \chi(b_0)^p + \chi(b_1)p \pmod{p^2} \\
\chi(c_0)^{p^2} + \chi(c_1)^pp + \chi(c_2)p^2 &\equiv \chi(a_0)^{p^2} + \chi(a_1)^pp + \chi(a_2)p^2  + \chi(b_0)^{p^2} + \chi(b_1)^pp + \chi(b_2)p^2 \pmod{p^3} \\
&\vdots
\end{align*}


Reducing modulo $p$ immediately yields \[ c_0 = a_0 + b_0 \quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_p.\]

And since $\chi(c_0)\equiv x+y \pmod p$, the difference \[x+y-\chi(c_0)\]
is divisible by $p$. Hence there exists a unique $u_1\in\mathbb{Z}_p$ such that \[x+y-\chi(c_0)=p\,u_1.\]

The element $u_1$ represents the carry to the next digit. We get that \[x+y-\chi(c_0)=pu_1=p\chi(c_1)+p^2\chi(c_2)+\cdots= p\bigl(\chi(c_1)+p\chi(c_2)+\cdots\bigr)\] 
\[\implies u_1 = \chi(c_1) + p\chi(c_2)+\cdots\]
And the second component satisfies \[\chi(c_1)\equiv c_1 \equiv u_1 \pmod p.\]

Now expanding the expression for $x+y-\chi(c_0)$ gives \[x+y-\chi(c_0) = \chi(a_0)+\chi(b_0)-\chi(a_0+b_0) + p(\chi(a_1)+\chi(b_1))+\cdots\]

The term\[\chi(a_0)+\chi(b_0)-\chi(a_0+b_0)\]
is always divisible by $p$, and therefore contributes to the carry term. Using the property of Teichmüller lifts
\[\chi(t)^p=\chi(t)\]
one obtains, modulo $p^2$, the identity \[c_0^p + p c_1=(a_0^p + p a_1) + (b_0^p + p b_1).\]

This suggests introducing the quantity \[w_1(a_0,a_1)=a_0^p + p a_1\]
for which the addition formula becomes \[w_1(c_0,c_1)=w_1(a_0,a_1)+w_1(b_0,b_1).\]

In other words, although the coordinates $(a_0,a_1)$ themselves interact through a complicated carry term, the quantity $w_1$ behaves additively.

This observation suggests that the arithmetic might simplify if, instead of working with the coefficients $(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)$ directly, one considers suitable polynomial expressions in these coefficients.

Continuing this reasoning leads naturally to the family of polynomials
\[w_n(a_0,a_1,\ldots,a_n) = a_0^{p^n} +p a_1^{p^{\,n-1}} + p^2 a_2^{p^{\,n-2}}+\cdots+p^n a_n\]
which are known as the \emph{Witt polynomials}. The values \[w_n(a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)\] are called the \emph{ghost components} of the sequence.

The key property of these quantities is that, when the operations on sequences are defined appropriately, the ghost components behave like ordinary ring coordinates:
\[ w_n(a+b)=w_n(a)+w_n(b), \qquad w_n(ab)=w_n(a)w_n(b)\]

Thus the complicated carry interactions in the Witt coordinates disappear when expressed in terms of the ghost components. This observation is the starting point of Witt's construction and will allow us to define addition and multiplication on sequences over an arbitrary ring.
